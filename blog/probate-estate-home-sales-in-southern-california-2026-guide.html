Selling an Inherited Home in Southern California: A 2026 Probate & Estate Sale Guide

If you are reading this, there is a reasonable chance you recently lost a parent, sibling, or spouse, and a Southern California home is now part of the responsibility on your shoulders. Grief and paperwork rarely arrive on convenient timelines, and the court process that follows a death can feel impenetrable when a property is involved and family members are looking to you for direction.

This guide covers the questions Paul Fernandez fields most often from heirs and personal representatives in Los Angeles, Orange, Riverside, and San Bernardino counties: when probate is required in California, how the process works, what it costs, and how a real estate sale fits inside it. The aim is a clearer picture before you make decisions you may not be able to reverse.

When Is Probate Required in California?

California sets two thresholds. As of 2026, estates with personal property under $184,500 may qualify for a small-estate affidavit. For real property valued at $750,000 or less, a Petition to Determine Succession to Real Property may be available. Above those numbers, formal probate is required unless an exception below applies.

Property Held in a Living Trust

Property held in a properly funded revocable living trust passes to beneficiaries without probate. The successor trustee handles the sale.

Joint Tenancy With a Surviving Joint Tenant

Joint tenancy with right of survivorship transfers full ownership to the surviving owner automatically. An Affidavit of Death of Joint Tenant is recorded.

Community Property With Right of Survivorship

For married couples titling property this way, the surviving spouse takes ownership automatically. The tax treatment often differs from joint tenancy, and a CPA or estate attorney can confirm which structure fits your situation.

Spousal Property Petition

A Spousal Property Petition (Form DE-221) can transfer title to a surviving spouse for community or quasi-community property in a streamlined hearing rather than full probate.

Revocable Transfer on Death Deed

California's RTOD deed names a beneficiary who takes the property at death without probate. It is authorized through January 1, 2032 and must be recorded during the owner's lifetime; have an attorney confirm validity.

Heggstad Petition

If a decedent meant to place property into their trust but never retitled it, a Heggstad petition (filed under Probate Code §850) can ask the court to confirm it as a trust asset. It is faster than full probate.

If none of these apply, formal probate is the path forward, and the rest of this article assumes that.

How California Probate Works (and How Long It Takes)

A probate petition is filed in the Superior Court of the county where the decedent resided at death, not where the property is located. A Whittier resident with a Temecula vacation home files in Los Angeles County. Once the court issues Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration, the personal representative can act.

A typical Southern California probate runs 9 to 18 months from filing to final distribution. The fastest cases rarely close in under 7 to 8 months due to statutory waiting periods. Court congestion, creditor claims, and family disputes extend that range.

What Probate Costs in California

California Probate Code §10810 sets statutory fees for the attorney and the personal representative. Each receives the full schedule, calculated on gross estate value, not net equity. Mortgages do not reduce the fee base. Confirm with your probate attorney.

Estate value bracket Statutory fee rate
First $100,000 4%
Next $100,000 3%
Next $800,000 2%
Next $9,000,000 1%
Next $15,000,000 0.5%
Above $25,000,000 Court-determined

For a $900,000 home (typical in Paul's area), each party's statutory fee is $21,000, so the estate pays $42,000 combined.

Additional expenses: court filing fee of $435 to $465 per petition (varies by county), referee appraisal of about 0.1% of estate value, newspaper publication of the Notice to Administer Estate at $200 to $400 for the three-week run, a bond premium if not waived, plus certified copies and recording fees.

Total cost on a $900,000 estate runs $45,000 to $55,000 before sale costs.

Selling Probate Real Estate: Full Authority vs. Limited Authority

The Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA) grants the personal representative one of two authority levels at the first hearing.

Full Authority (Independent Administration)

The personal representative can accept an offer and sell without prior court approval. After acceptance, the attorney files a Notice of Proposed Action (Form DE-165), and heirs have 15 days to object. If no one objects, escrow proceeds normally. Heirs can sign a Waiver of Notice (Form DE-166) to skip the wait. Most probate attorneys request full authority at the first hearing.

Limited Authority (Court-Supervised)

Limited authority adds court supervision. Price must be at least 90% of the probate referee's reappraised value, with a 10% earnest money deposit. The sale must be published three times in a local newspaper over a minimum 10-day window, the final at least a week after the first. A confirmation hearing follows 30 days after Form DE-260 is filed. Overbidding is allowed: minimum is the original price plus 10% of the first $10,000 plus 5% of the remainder. A $526,000 bid requires $552,800.

A Realtor familiar with probate is often the difference between a 60-day escrow and a six-month one.

When You Should Bring in a Probate Real Estate Specialist

The probate referee's appraised value sets the floor; under limited authority it is the legal minimum. An experienced probate Realtor provides the referee with comps and condition documentation, producing accurate valuation that protects the personal representative from later disputes.

During marketing, probate buyers expect specific disclosures and contract language. A non-specialist often loses qualified buyers because terms are wrong from the start.

A Practical Checklist for Personal Representatives

  1. Locate the original death certificate and will (if one exists); order at least five certified copies from the county recorder.
  2. Identify how title is held: grant deed, joint tenancy, trust, or RTOD.
  3. Determine whether probate is required or whether a statutory alternative applies.
  4. Engage a California probate attorney before filing anything.
  5. File the petition (Form DE-111) in the county where the decedent resided.
  6. Schedule the probate referee appraisal soon after Letters are issued.
  7. Secure the property: change locks, maintain insurance, photograph condition.
  8. Begin Realtor consultations early; do not wait until Letters are in hand.
  9. Track all out-of-pocket expenses for reimbursement at final accounting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to use a probate-specialist Realtor, or can I use any agent?

Any licensed Realtor can list a probate property, but the procedural differences matter. A non-specialist may delay confirmation hearings, mishandle the Notice of Proposed Action, or accept an offer below the 90% reappraised-value threshold under limited authority. The cost is measured in months that compound into carrying costs the estate absorbs.

Can I sell the home before probate is filed?

No. Until the court issues Letters of Administration or Letters Testamentary (Form DE-150), no one has legal authority to transfer title. Listing for marketing is sometimes done in parallel with filing, but the sale cannot close until Letters are in hand and, under full authority, the Notice of Proposed Action period has run.

How is probate different in Los Angeles County versus Orange, Riverside, or San Bernardino County?

The substantive law is identical statewide. The differences are practical: court calendar speed, clerk responsiveness, local rules on tentative rulings, and the pool of probate referees assigned to each courthouse. Los Angeles County tends to have the longest waits to first hearing because of volume; the other three counties typically move faster.
